Policies

Policies anchor the framework. They should define what tracking is, what it is for, where boundaries sit, and when review is required. The point is to create usable rules that teams can apply consistently.

Purpose

Set the baseline for how your organization approaches tracking, privacy, and external data-sharing decisions.

Operational Rules

Turn policy language into practical boundaries that marketing, product, and engineering can actually follow.

Governance

Clarify what requires review, who owns escalation, and how exceptions should be handled.

Purpose

This part should explain what the policy is doing inside the broader Pixelwise system. It is the rule layer. It gives teams a shared baseline before requests start flowing.

Scope

The policy should define what counts as tracking, what tools are in scope, and what kinds of environments matter most when applying the framework.

Tracking technologies in scope

Cookies, pixels, SDKs, APIs, session replay, analytics tools, and other mechanisms that collect or transmit user interaction data.

Environment matters

Public pages, pre-login flows, and authenticated experiences should not all be treated the same.

Core Rules

This is where the working rules live: approved use cases, restricted uses, third-party sharing expectations, and transparency requirements.

Governance

A strong policy should make clear what requires legal or privacy review, what can move faster, and how decisions get documented.

Playbooks

Playbooks are the decision layer. They translate policy into action by helping teams evaluate real requests in a consistent way instead of improvising every time.

Use Cases

Start with the business need, not the vendor name. The playbook should clarify what outcome is actually being pursued.

Decision Flow

Guide users through data involved, environment, vendor role, likely risk, and mitigation options.

Cross-Functional Use

Make it easier for legal, product, marketing, and engineering to move together around one structured model.

How They Work

Playbooks should create repeatability. Same categories of request should move through the same basic structure.

Marketing Requests

Campaign measurement, attribution, vendor pixels, audience targeting, and consent-sensitive uses should be handled here.

Product Requests

Product analytics, feature usage monitoring, onboarding flows, and experimentation should be framed within the same logic.

Engineering Requests

Engineering should get concrete implementation guidance, not vague instructions to “be careful.”

Contract Templates

Contract templates should support the framework, not replace the analysis. They help align vendor relationships with the actual data flow and approved use case.

Purpose

Clarify what contracts do and do not solve inside a tracking or privacy program.

Vendor Logic

Help teams map vendor roles, permitted uses, restrictions, and downstream sharing boundaries.

Fallback Language

Give users language that helps control risk when vendor paper is broad or implementation details are still moving.

Purpose

Contracts govern relationships. They do not independently determine whether data should flow the way a team proposes.

BAA / DPA Logic

Users should understand when a BAA or DPA matters and when invoking one is not enough.

Vendor Boundaries

This section should clarify secondary use, retention, aggregation, downstream vendors, and implementation assumptions.

Fallback Clauses

Fallback language should help users narrow scope, preserve control, and align the paper with actual operations.

Risk Spectrums

Risk spectrums give users a more useful way to evaluate tracking and privacy decisions than simple yes/no answers. They show where something sits and what moves it higher or lower.

Lower Risk

Minimal data, strong contextual fit, tight controls, and limited third-party exposure.

Managed / Gray Zone

Practical use cases that may be acceptable with clear boundaries, minimization, and mitigation.

High Risk

Context-rich or identifiable data flows, weak alignment with expectations, and heavy external sharing.

Why Spectrums

Most real-world uses do not sit at an obvious yes or no. A spectrum helps teams see tradeoffs more honestly.
